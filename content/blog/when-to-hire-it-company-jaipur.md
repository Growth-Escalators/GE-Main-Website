Most Jaipur businesses don't "decide" to hire an IT company. They stumble into it after the third server outage mid-invoice-cycle, or after a laptop gets ransomware because nobody owned patching. Reactive IT works fine until it doesn't — then you're paying for the fix at twice the cost of prevention.

Here's how to tell if you've outgrown ad-hoc IT, what a managed IT company actually covers versus a full-time hire, and the real ₹ numbers for both paths.

## Signs you've outgrown ad-hoc IT

Ad-hoc IT — a cousin who's "good with computers," a freelancer on emergency retainer, or whoever's least afraid of Windows settings — works fine for a five-person shop with one shared printer. It stops working when any of these show up:

- **You've had downtime that cost real money**, not just annoyance. A billing system down for four hours during month-end close is a different category of problem than a printer jam.
- **You store anything a regulator, bank, or client would care about** — customer data, payment records, signed contracts. "We'll deal with security later" stops being viable once there's something worth stealing.
- **Nobody can tell you when the last backup actually ran**, let alone when it was last tested by a real restore. An unverified backup is a belief, not a backup.
- **IT decisions get made by whoever's available**, with no coherent view of your systems — software bought ad hoc, nothing talks to anything else, troubleshooting is guesswork.
- **You're growing past 15-20 people** or opening a second location. Ad-hoc IT scales linearly with headaches; past a certain size, headaches compound.
- **Compliance or a client is now asking questions** — a bank wants your security posture before signing, or a client's procurement team sends a questionnaire you can't answer.

Two or more true means ad-hoc IT already costs more than a real IT relationship would.

## What a managed IT company actually covers

"Managed IT" gets used loosely. A real managed IT engagement in 2026 should cover:

- **Hosting and infrastructure management** — servers, cloud environments, domain/DNS, email infrastructure, kept configured and patched.
- **Uptime monitoring** — someone, or something automated, watching whether systems are actually up, with alerts before customers notice.
- **Security** — firewall configuration, endpoint protection, patch management, and basic staff security awareness (phishing remains the #1 way small businesses get breached).
- **Backups — tested, not assumed.** Automated, off-site, and periodically test-restored so a broken backup surfaces during a drill, not a disaster.
- **Helpdesk** — a real channel (not a WhatsApp number ignored on weekends) covering everything from "laptop won't connect" to "is this email a phishing attempt."
- **Vendor and license management** — tracking what software you pay for, whether you're over-licensed, and whether renewals are actually handled.
- **A documented environment** — network diagrams, access lists, system documentation — so the business doesn't stall if the one person who "knows how everything works" leaves.

A vendor that only shows up when something breaks is break-fix support wearing a managed-services label — reactive and priced per incident, versus proactive and priced as a relationship.

## Managed IT vs in-house hire: the real cost math

Most founders get this comparison wrong by pitting a managed IT retainer against one junior hire's salary and concluding the hire is cheaper. It usually isn't, once everything is counted.

| Cost factor | Managed IT company | In-house IT hire |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly cost (typical Jaipur SMB, 15-50 employees) | ₹15,000 – ₹60,000/month retainer | ₹35,000 – ₹90,000/month salary (1 person) |
| Coverage hours | Often 24/7 or extended-hours SLA | Standard work hours only, unless you pay for on-call |
| Skill breadth | Team covers networking, security, cloud, helpdesk | One person's skill set — gaps show up under a real incident |
| Sick leave / vacation / attrition risk | None — team continuity is the vendor's problem | Real risk — one person out sick during an outage means no coverage |
| Tooling and monitoring stack | Included — vendor already has monitoring, ticketing, security tools | You buy and maintain the tooling yourself, on top of salary |
| Scaling with growth | Retainer scales with usage/scope | Requires hiring a second, third person as you grow |
| Best fit | Businesses without complex, proprietary infra needs | Businesses with heavy day-to-day, on-site hardware/ops needs |

The pattern: for most SMBs under roughly 50 employees without heavy daily on-site hardware, a managed IT retainer beats one in-house generalist hire on cost, coverage, and skill breadth at once. The math flips once you're large enough to justify a multi-person internal team — then in-house wins on responsiveness and institutional knowledge.

## Decision table: what fits your situation

| Your situation | Recommended path |
|---|---|
| Under 20 employees, no dedicated IT today | Managed IT — you can't justify a full-time hire's cost yet |
| 20-75 employees, standard office/SaaS stack | Managed IT — broader skill coverage than one hire, lower cost |
| Heavy daily on-site hardware (manufacturing, labs, clinics with imaging equipment) | Hybrid — in-house for hands-on hardware, managed for infra/security/backups |
| Multiple locations across Jaipur or beyond | Managed IT with centralized monitoring — one internal hire can't be in two places |
| 100+ employees, complex custom infrastructure | In-house team, possibly supplemented by managed for after-hours/overflow |
| Regulated data (finance, healthcare) and no security posture today | Managed IT immediately — this is not a "get to it eventually" item |
| You already have one strong internal IT person who's drowning | Hybrid — managed IT takes routine monitoring/patching/helpdesk off their plate so they focus on strategic work |

## What to look for in a managed IT company

Not all managed IT vendors are equal — this is the checklist we'd want any [IT company in Jaipur](/it-company-in-jaipur) to pass before you sign anything:

- **A written SLA with actual numbers** — response time for critical issues (under 1 hour for anything business-stopping), resolution targets, uptime guarantees. "We'll get to it" is not an SLA.
- **Real after-hours coverage.** Ask what happens if a server goes down at 11pm Saturday. "Someone might see the email Monday" isn't 24/7 support regardless of the brochure.
- **Security-first posture** — specifically how they handle patch management, backup testing, and incident response, not just whether they claim to.
- **Transparent, itemized pricing and callable references** — exactly what's billed extra, and a same-size-industry client you can actually phone.
- **A plan for onboarding your existing mess.** Most businesses arrive without clean documentation. A credible vendor has a defined 30-day audit process, not a vague promise to "figure it out."
