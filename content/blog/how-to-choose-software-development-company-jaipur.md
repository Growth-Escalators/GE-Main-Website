Picking a software development company is a worse decision to get wrong than most vendor choices, because a bad one doesn't show up on day one. It shows up eight months in, when the codebase is unmaintainable, the original developer has left, and you're choosing between an expensive rebuild and living with something broken.

This isn't a pricing breakdown, and it isn't a build-vs-buy argument — those are separate questions. This is the checklist for the vendor decision itself: how to scope before you shop, how to read a proposal, what to ask, and the red flags that predict a bad outcome before you've signed anything.

## Scope before you shop

The single biggest mistake founders make is calling vendors before writing down what they actually need. Vague requests get vague quotes, and vague quotes are unenforceable once scope creep hits.

Before your first call:

- **Write down the core user roles** and the 3-5 things each must do on day one. The must-have list, not the wishlist.
- **List required integrations** — payment gateway, SMS/WhatsApp, CRM, accounting software. Every integration is a scope item, not an afterthought.
- **Decide your timeline constraint** and say so upfront.
- **Know your budget range**, even roughly. Hiding it doesn't get a better price — it gets a proposal that doesn't fit your constraints.

A one-page brief with this information gets dramatically better, more comparable quotes than a phone call where you describe the idea from memory.

## Fixed-scope vs sprint retainer — which to ask for

Every legitimate vendor quotes one of two ways.

**Fixed-scope quote.** A locked price against a locked spec. Works when requirements are genuinely settled. Breaks down when they're still moving — every change becomes a change order, and that's where fixed-scope projects blow past budget and goodwill.

**Sprint retainer.** You pay for a fixed amount of development capacity per week or month and prioritize the backlog together as you go. Suits MVPs and anything where you won't know the right next feature until real users touch version one — most software.

Ask which model is being proposed and why. A vendor defaulting to fixed-scope for an undefined product is either inexperienced or setting up a change-order fight. One insisting on retainer-only for a fully specified project may be optimizing for open-ended billing.

## Who owns the code — get this in writing before you start

This should be a non-negotiable contract line item — the one founders skip most often because it feels like a formality until it isn't.

- **You should own the IP outright** — source code, designs, custom assets — once final payment clears. Not a license to use it; ownership.
- **Get repository access during the build**, not just at handover. Seeing commits land in a repo you control proves work is progressing and means you're never locked out.
- **Check for third-party licensing traps** — some vendors build on proprietary frameworks they own, so you don't own the full stack even after paying. Ask: "Could a different team pick up this codebase today?" If the answer is no, find out why before signing.

## Post-launch support and maintenance — ask before, not after

A quote for "the build" is not a quote for "the software, forever," and this is where relationships sour. Ask specifically:

- **What's covered in the post-launch warranty, and for how long?** Bug fixes from the original build should be free for a defined window (30-90 days typical) — new features are not.
- **What does ongoing maintenance cost, and is it optional?** Security patches aren't optional in practice, even when sold as an add-on.
- **What's the response time** for a broken production issue versus a minor bug versus a feature request? A serious vendor already has this defined.
- **What happens if you leave after launch?** A vendor withholding documentation or writing intentionally unhandoverable code is building in lock-in — a red flag on its own.

## In-house Jaipur team vs. cheap offshore — the trade-off nobody quotes you

The lowest bid you'll get is almost always offshore or a solo freelancer platform, priced well below a local in-house shop. Sometimes that's a fine trade. Often it isn't, for reasons the quote doesn't show:

- **Timezone drag turns clarifying questions into day-long delays** — real slippage on a 10-12 week build.
- **Handoff quality varies wildly** with freelancers who move platforms — undocumented code becomes expensive archaeology later.
- **Accountability is thin** with no local presence or reputational stake in your market.
- **Rework eats the discount.** Underpriced builds are underpriced because something got cut — tests, error handling, scale planning. Not cheaper if it needs a partial rebuild in year two.

## How to actually read a portfolio

Portfolios are marketing material, and most founders read them the wrong way — scrolling screenshots and judging visual polish. Instead:

- **Ask for a project similar in scope to yours**, not the flashiest one on the page. A five-page marketing site doesn't prove a vendor can build a multi-role booking system.
- **Ask what specifically that team built** versus what the client's own team or another vendor contributed.
- **Ask to see something still live and running**, not a static case study — click through it. Broken links on a "featured project" are telling.
- **Ask what went wrong on a past project.** A vendor claiming a flawless track record either hasn't shipped much or isn't being straight with you.

## Red flags to walk away from

| Red flag | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| No discovery phase — quote arrives within hours | Real scoping takes time; instant quotes mean guesswork |
| Vague, unitemized pricing ("₹3-8L depending") | You can't compare quotes or hold anyone accountable |
| No mention of code ownership or IP terms | You may not own what you're paying for |
| No defined post-launch support window | Expect a fight over "bug vs. new feature" after launch |
| Reluctant to give repo access during the build | No visibility into whether work is actually progressing |
| Can't name a comparable past project | They may be learning on your budget |
| Pressure to sign same-day for a "discount" | Urgency tactics are a sales pattern, not scoping |
| Unclear who's actually coding vs. selling | You want a senior developer on it, not just a salesperson |
