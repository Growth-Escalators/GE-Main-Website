Every founder eventually asks the same question in a different form: "Should we just buy [Software X], or build our own?" The honest answer is that neither default is right. Off-the-shelf and custom software solve different problems, and choosing wrong is expensive in both directions — buying when you needed custom locks you into someone else's roadmap; building when you needed to buy burns months and lakhs reinventing a tool that costs a few thousand rupees a month.

Here's how to actually make the call.

## When off-the-shelf is the right call

Off-the-shelf (SaaS) wins when:

- **The problem is generic.** Accounting, email marketing, help desk, basic CRM, project management — thousands of companies have this exact problem, and mature products have had years of user feedback baked into them. You will not out-build Zoho or HubSpot on a generic CRM.
- **Speed matters more than fit.** You can be live on a SaaS tool in a day. A custom build, even a fast one, takes weeks at minimum.
- **You don't yet know your exact workflow.** Early-stage businesses often don't know what they actually need until they've used something for six months. Renting first is cheaper than guessing wrong in code.
- **The vendor's roadmap roughly matches yours.** If the tool is actively adding the features you'd want next, you're borrowing their R&D budget for free.

## When custom pays off

Custom software wins when:

- **Your workflow is the differentiator.** If the way you run bookings, price jobs, or route leads is actually your competitive edge, running it on a generic tool means that edge is available to every competitor using the same SaaS.
- **You're stitching together four-plus tools with duct tape.** Once you're paying for five subscriptions and building Zapier chains to connect them, you're already paying for "almost custom" — badly. A unified system is often cheaper and more reliable.
- **The SaaS pricing model punishes your scale.** Per-seat or per-contact pricing that looked fine at 500 users can become a six-figure annual bill at 50,000. Custom has a ceiling; SaaS often doesn't.
- **Data ownership and control actually matter.** Healthcare, fintech, and anything with compliance requirements often can't accept "trust the vendor" as an answer.
- **You need something that doesn't exist yet**, or exists but is built for a market that isn't yours — a US-built tool that doesn't handle GST, UPI, or regional-language support properly, for example.

## The real number: 3-year total cost of ownership

The sticker-price comparison — "₹2,000/month vs. ₹5,00,000 to build" — is the wrong comparison. Run it over three years and include what SaaS pricing pages don't show you.

| Cost factor | Off-the-shelf (SaaS) | Custom software |
|---|---|---|
| Upfront cost | Low (₹0–₹50,000 setup) | High (₹2,00,000–₹15,00,000+) |
| Monthly cost at current scale | ₹5,000–₹50,000+ | ₹5,000–₹20,000 (hosting/infra) |
| Cost as you scale (users/data/volume) | Rises with usage — often steeply | Flat-ish — infra scales cheaper than per-seat SaaS fees |
| Customization | Limited to vendor's roadmap and settings | Whatever you need, whenever you need it |
| Integration with your other systems | Depends on vendor's API and partners | Built exactly for your stack |
| Switching cost later | High — data migration, retraining, lock-in | Low — you own the code and data |
| Realistic 3-year cost at 3–5x current scale | Often ₹15L–₹40L+ (mid-market SaaS at scale) | Build cost plus ~15–20%/year maintenance |

The crossover point — where custom becomes cheaper than SaaS — usually shows up faster than founders expect, especially for per-seat tools once headcount or transaction volume triples.

## Four levers that actually decide it

Beyond cost, four factors usually settle the argument:

- **Speed.** SaaS wins here, always. If you need something live this week, build isn't an option regardless of the other factors.
- **Control.** Custom wins. You decide the roadmap, not a product manager at a company you've never met who might deprecate the feature you depend on.
- **Integrations.** Depends. If the SaaS tool has a genuinely open API and your other tools are mainstream, integration is a non-issue. If you're in a niche vertical, custom often integrates better because it's built to.
- **Lock-in.** SaaS creates it — exporting years of workflow rules, automations, and historical data out of a platform is rarely clean. Custom has no lock-in because you own the code and the data outright.

## Decision table: your situation → build or buy

| Your situation | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Early-stage, testing a new process, under 10 employees | Buy — rent until the workflow is proven |
| Workflow is generic (accounting, email, basic CRM) | Buy — don't reinvent it |
| Workflow is your competitive edge | Build |
| Already paying for four-plus tools glued together with Zapier | Build a unified system |
| SaaS pricing scales badly with your growth | Build, or renegotiate hard first |
| Compliance or data-residency requirements | Build, or vet the vendor's compliance very carefully |
| Need something that fits Indian workflows (GST, UPI, regional language) that global SaaS doesn't handle well | Build |
| Need it live in days, not weeks | Buy |

## The hybrid path most businesses actually land on

In practice, very few businesses are 100% build or 100% buy. The pattern we see most often working with clients at [Growth Escalators's software company in Jaipur](/software-company-in-jaipur): run the generic, undifferentiated stuff on SaaS — accounting, email infrastructure, basic project management — and build custom only for the layer that's actually the business: the booking engine, the lead-scoring logic, the client portal, the internal ops tool nobody else needs because nobody else runs your specific workflow. Then connect the two with clean APIs instead of Zapier duct tape once volume justifies it.

That's also how we run our own agency — SaaS for accounting and email, custom-built for the CRM, lead-scoring, and reporting tooling that actually differentiates how we run client accounts day to day.
