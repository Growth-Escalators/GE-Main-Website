## What actually drives the cost

Four variables move the number more than anything else.

**1. Scope and complexity.** A five-page marketing site with a contact form is a different animal from a multi-role booking system with payments, notifications, and an admin dashboard. Every screen, every user role, every "if this then that" business rule adds development and testing time.

**2. Integrations.** Payment gateways (Razorpay, Stripe), SMS/WhatsApp APIs, CRMs, accounting software, third-party booking engines — each one is a mini-project of its own: reading docs, handling auth, handling failure states, testing edge cases. Two integrations can add 30-40% to a build.

**3. Stack and technical debt tolerance.** A modern stack (React/Next.js frontend, Node.js backend, managed Postgres) costs more upfront in senior-developer time than a WordPress-plus-plugins stack, but it doesn't accumulate the same maintenance debt. Cheap-now stacks are frequently expensive-later stacks.

**4. Team seniority.** A junior developer and a senior architect can both "build the same feature" — one of them ships it without a security hole, a scalable data model, and code the next developer can actually read. You're not paying for lines of code; you're paying for judgment.

## Fixed-scope quote vs. sprint retainer

You'll get quoted one of two ways. Both are legitimate; they suit different situations.

**Fixed-scope quote** — you get a locked price for a locked spec. Good when you know exactly what you're building (a defined website, a defined booking flow). Bad when requirements are still moving — every change request becomes a change order, and change orders are where fixed-scope projects blow past budget and timeline.

**Sprint retainer** — you pay for a fixed amount of dev capacity per week or month (e.g., ₹1,50,000/month for a 2-person pod) and prioritize the backlog together. Good for MVPs, evolving products, and anything where "we'll know the right feature after we ship the first version" is true — which, honestly, is most software.

Our default recommendation: fixed-scope for the first release if the spec is genuinely locked, retainer for anything after that.

## In-house Jaipur team vs. cheap offshore: the hidden costs

The lowest quote you'll get for a custom build usually comes from an offshore team or a solo freelancer platform, priced 30–50% below a local in-house shop. It's tempting. It's also where most of the horror stories in this category come from. The costs that don't show up in the quote:

- **Timezone drag.** A 5.5-hour-plus gap means every clarifying question costs a day, not an hour. On a 12-week build, that's real calendar time lost.
- **Handoff quality.** Freelancers move on. If the code isn't documented and the architecture isn't sane, the next developer — often you, six months later, hiring someone else — pays to re-learn it before they can touch it.
- **No real accountability.** A one-person contractor with your ₹3L advance and no local presence has very little to lose if the relationship goes sideways. A team you can walk into in Sanganer does.
- **Rework.** The single biggest hidden cost. Underpriced builds are underpriced because corners got cut — no tests, no error handling, no thought given to what happens at 10x the current data volume. The "cheap" ₹2L build that needs a ₹3L rebuild eighteen months later wasn't cheap.

## Post-launch maintenance — the line item everyone forgets

A quote for "the build" is not a quote for "the software, forever." Budget for:

- **Hosting and infra** — ₹2,000–₹15,000/month depending on traffic and services (this scales with usage, not one-time)
- **Security patches and dependency updates** — skipping this is how a two-year-old app becomes unmaintainable or gets breached
- **Small feature requests and bug fixes** — either a retainer (₹15,000–₹60,000/month for a few hours a week) or a pay-per-ticket arrangement
- **Monitoring** — error tracking and uptime alerts; cheap to set up, expensive to not have when something breaks silently

Plan for maintenance to run 15–20% of the build cost per year. If nobody quoted you that, ask why.

## How to scope a project to actually control cost

The lever that moves price the most isn't negotiating the hourly rate — it's controlling scope before development starts.

- **Write down the user roles and the 3–5 things each role must be able to do.** Everything beyond that is v2.
- **Decide integrations upfront**, not mid-build. Adding a payment gateway in week 8 of a 10-week project is expensive; scoping it in week 1 is not.
- **Separate "must ship" from "nice to have"** and get a quote against the must-ship list only.
- **Ask for a phased roadmap** instead of one giant spec — ship the core loop, learn from real usage, and fund phase 2 with what you learned instead of guessing upfront.
