Search "b2b lead generation companies in india" and you'll get fifty agencies making the same three claims: qualified pipeline, proven process, real results. Almost none of them explain their pricing model until you're three calls deep, and even fewer will define what they mean by "lead" in writing.

That's a bad way to hand someone your outbound motion, your ICP data, and your CAC. This is a buyer's guide — not a ranked list — for evaluating a B2B lead generation partner in India before you sign anything.

## What "B2B lead generation" actually covers

"Lead generation" gets used as a catch-all for at least five distinct services, and the vendor you need depends on which ones you're actually buying:

- **Appointment setting / SDR-as-a-service** — a dedicated or shared SDR (sales development rep) team prospects, qualifies, and books meetings straight onto your sales team's calendar.
- **LinkedIn outbound** — connection requests, InMail sequences, and content-led engagement aimed at named accounts or job titles.
- **Cold email** — multi-touch sequences run through dedicated sending infrastructure, usually paired with intent or enrichment data.
- **Telesales / telecalling** — outbound calling for qualification, discovery, or direct closing; common for lower-ACV or high-volume B2B products.
- **Paid demand gen** — LinkedIn Ads, Google Search, and retargeting built to capture inbound form-fills rather than outbound prospecting.

Most credible agencies blend two or three of these. Single-channel outbound — LinkedIn-only, say — tends to plateau within a couple of quarters, because the same accounts are getting hit by every other vendor running the identical playbook.

## Pricing models — and how to judge each one

Three pricing models dominate the Indian market, and each one creates a different incentive for the agency running it.

| Model | How it works | Typical India pricing | Optimizes for | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pay-per-lead | You pay per contact/MQL delivered | ₹500–₹3,000 per lead, depending on seniority and vertical | Volume | "Lead" can mean a scraped email address, not a real prospect |
| Retainer | Fixed monthly fee for a dedicated SDR pod | ₹60,000–₹2,50,000/month for 1–3 SDRs | Activity and pipeline | Easy to pay for effort instead of outcomes if it isn't tracked |
| Pay-per-meeting / performance | You pay per qualified, booked meeting | ₹3,000–₹15,000 per meeting, depending on ICP seniority and deal size | Qualified pipeline | "Qualified" needs a written, contractual definition |
| Dedicated telesales | Outsourced calling team, billed per agent or per call | ₹25,000–₹60,000 per agent/month | Call volume and conversion | Script quality and DND compliance vary a lot between vendors |

No model is inherently better than the others — a pay-per-meeting deal with a loose "qualified" definition is worse than a well-run retainer with weekly reporting. The pricing model matters less than what's contractually defined as success.

## Lead quality is the real differentiator — not the price per lead

This is the single biggest factor separating agencies worth paying from agencies quietly burning your budget: do you receive booked, qualified meetings with people who match your ideal customer profile (ICP) — or a spreadsheet of names you still have to work yourself?

A contact list is not a lead. Anyone can export 5,000 "VP of Operations, 200–1,000 employees" contacts from a data tool for a few thousand rupees. The value an agency should add is qualification — confirming budget, authority, need, or timeline (or a lighter version of it) before the meeting lands on your sales calendar.

Ask any shortlisted vendor for their qualification criteria in writing, and what percentage of last quarter's booked meetings turned into a second call. An agency that can't answer fast is reselling a contact list with better formatting.

## Transparency, tooling, and compliance

Three operational questions reveal a lot about how a vendor actually runs day to day:

- **Do they give you CRM access, or just a weekly report?** Live pipeline visibility — HubSpot, Zoho, even a shared sheet updated in real time — beats a polished PDF every Friday.
- **What powers their outbound?** Sales Navigator, verified email-sending infrastructure (separate domains, proper warmup, SPF/DKIM/DMARC configured), and a dialer with call recording are the baseline. If a vendor can't name their stack, they're probably doing it manually and inconsistently.
- **Are they DND- and deliverability-compliant?** Telecalling into India's DND (Do Not Disturb) registry without scrubbing your list becomes your compliance problem the moment a complaint is filed. Cold email sent from your primary domain without warmup can tank deliverability for months. Ask where they send from and how they protect your domain reputation.

## In-house SDR vs. agency: the real cost math

Founders often assume one in-house SDR is cheaper than an agency retainer. The fully-loaded number rarely supports that assumption.

| Cost component | In-house SDR | Agency retainer (1–3 SDRs) |
|---|---|---|
| Base cost | ₹35,000–₹60,000/month salary | ₹60,000–₹2,50,000/month, all-in |
| Tools (Sales Navigator, email infra, dialer, data) | ₹20,000–₹35,000/month, billed to you | Included |
| Management overhead | Your time or a sales manager's | Included |
| Ramp time to full productivity | 60–90 days, largely unproductive | Days, not months — infrastructure already built |
| Attrition risk | High in SDR roles (12–18 month average tenure) | Absorbed by the vendor |

In-house wins when you're building a long-term revenue org and want the SDR function owned internally past 12 months. Agency wins when you need pipeline now, want to test a message or ICP before committing headcount, or don't have the management bandwidth to run a junior SDR day to day. Many B2B companies run both — an agency for testing and overflow, in-house once the motion is proven.

## Red flags to walk away from

- Guarantees a specific number of "leads" without defining what qualifies as one
- Won't name the industries or company sizes they've actually booked meetings for — "B2B experience" alone isn't proof of fit
- Refuses call recordings or CRM access during the engagement
- Runs cold email from your primary company domain, risking your deliverability
- No mention of DND compliance when telecalling is part of the scope
- Locks you into a 12-month contract with no early-exit or performance clause
- Can't explain their qualification process beyond "we vet the leads"

## Vendor evaluation checklist

Run every shortlisted agency through this before you sign anything:

| Question to ask | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| What's your exact definition of a "qualified" lead or meeting? | Prevents disputes later over what you're actually paying for |
| What CRM or dashboard will I have live access to? | Transparency test — real-time beats a weekly PDF |
| What's your show-up rate on booked meetings? | Reveals whether meetings are confirmed, not just scheduled |
| Which industries or ICPs have you booked meetings for in the last 6 months? | Generic "B2B experience" isn't proof of fit for your vertical |
| How do you protect deliverability and stay DND-compliant? | Compliance and domain reputation become your liability, not theirs |
| What's the contract term and exit clause? | Avoid lock-in with no performance recourse |
| Can I speak to a current client with a similar ICP? | Case studies get polished; references don't |
